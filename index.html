Dr. Marie Curie

Pioneer of Radioactivity and Two-Time Nobel Prize Winner

A Life Dedicated to Science

1867

Born in Warsaw

Maria Sklodowska was born in Warsaw, Poland, during a time when Poland was under Russian occupation. Despite financial hardships, her family valued education highly.

1891

Studies in Paris

Moved to Paris to study at the Sorbonne, where she earned degrees in physics and mathematics. She lived in poverty but was determined to pursue her scientific dreams.

1895

Marriage to Pierre Curie

Married Pierre Curie, a fellow scientist. Together they would form one of the most famous scientific partnerships in history.

1898

Discovery of Polonium and Radium

Along with Pierre, discovered two new elements: polonium (named after her homeland) and radium. This work laid the foundation for modern atomic physics.

1903

First Nobel Prize

Awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics (shared with Pierre Curie and Henri Becquerel) for their work on radioactivity, becoming the first woman to win a Nobel Prize.

1906

Tragedy and Perseverance

Pierre Curie died in a tragic accident. Marie continued their research alone and became the first female professor at the Sorbonne.

1911

Second Nobel Prize

Won the Nobel Prize in Chemistry for the discovery of radium and polonium, becoming the first person to win Nobel Prizes in two different scientific fields.

1914-1918

World War I Service

Developed mobile X-ray units ("petites Curies") to help battlefield surgeons, personally driving them to the front lines and saving countless lives.

1934

Legacy Lives On

Died from aplastic anemia, likely caused by radiation exposure. Her research notebooks are still radioactive and will remain so for another 1,500 years.

Lasting Impact

Marie Curie's groundbreaking research laid the foundation for modern atomic physics and chemistry. Her work on radioactivity opened new frontiers in medicine, leading to treatments for cancer and other diseases. Beyond her scientific contributions, she broke gender barriers in academia and inspired generations of women to pursue careers in science.

Today, the element curium (Cm) is named in honor of Marie and Pierre Curie, and the unit of radioactivity, the curie (Ci), commemorates their pioneering work. Her legacy continues through the Curie Institute in Paris, a leading cancer research center, and through the countless scientists she has inspired.
